# RDI Principal CRA Candidate Grading Rubric

## Purpose

Use this rubric to evaluate candidates for RDI's Principal CRA - Monitoring Standards & Training role based on their resume, application answers, written responses, interview notes, and practical monitoring exercise.

This role is not a traditional CRA role, Clinical Trial Manager role, people-management role, or SOP-writing engagement. It is a senior monitoring craft seat with a teaching mandate.

The core question is:

> **Can this person raise RDI's monitoring standard through real work, transfer judgment to the offshore team, and become less needed over time?**

The strongest candidate can do three things at once:

1. Maintain senior clinical-monitoring credibility through a small live caseload.
2. Teach monitoring judgment by reviewing real work side-by-side with the team.
3. Build a written, company-owned rubric and example library that outlasts any individual study or person.

---

## Role Context

RDI is an IVD-focused CRO running diagnostic studies for sponsors. The company is small, fast-moving, and execution-heavy. Monitoring quality matters because RDI's credibility depends on clean source review, protocol compliance, deviation judgment, query handling, site follow-up, sample integrity, instrument/data traceability, and inspection-ready documentation.

RDI has a hardworking offshore clinical operations team in the Philippines. They are intelligent, ambitious, high-output, and cost-effective. They also work in a different communication context than many US clinical monitors are used to. The right Principal CRA must be able to coach across culture, time zone, tone, and style without ego.

This is important: the candidate cannot be threatened by a strong offshore team. They must not interpret informality, directness, eagerness, humor, or different communication norms as lack of seriousness. They also must not become defensive because the team is hardworking, capable, and lower-cost. The role exists to transfer standards, not protect status.

The Principal CRA will own:

- RDI's monitoring standard
- Monitoring rubric and example library
- Review of real monitoring work
- Coaching and calibration with offshore CRAs / clinical operations staff
- Senior escalation on difficult monitoring judgment calls
- A small live monitoring caseload, usually around 25-35%
- Continuous improvement of monitoring practices
- Training that makes the team more independent over time
- IVD-specific monitoring judgment for sample collection, sample stability, storage conditions, chain of custody, instrument use, and data traceability

This role requires 3-4 hours of daily overlap with the offshore team in UTC+8, usually early US Pacific morning or late evening. That is a real fit gate.

---

## Core Hiring Thesis

RDI is not hiring a senior CRA to be the hero monitor. RDI is hiring a senior craftsperson who can turn individual monitoring expertise into a company standard.

The right candidate has deep monitoring judgment and genuine teaching instinct. They can review a real monitoring file, identify what matters, explain why it matters, show the team what good looks like, and turn the lesson into a durable rubric.

The candidate must be comfortable being a peer-expert to a self-managing offshore team. They are not there to manage workload, police effort, or prove seniority. They are there to raise judgment.

The strongest candidate earns authority through craft. Weak candidates rely on title, therapeutic-area name-dropping, SOP citations, rigid checklist behavior, or "I have been doing this for 20 years" energy.

RDI should be especially careful with candidates who have survived as traveling CRAs by mastering one protocol, following a checklist rigidly, and repeating the same type of monitoring visit for years. That background can produce useful discipline, but it can also hide low portability. RDI studies move quickly, vary by sponsor, and often require first-principles IVD thinking. A candidate who wants this role mainly to get out of travel, but cannot learn new study designs quickly, will struggle.

This is a role for a monitor who can think, teach, and adapt. It is not a retirement lane for a travel CRA who wants less travel but still needs a fixed playbook.

---

## Existing Team Context / Gap to Fill

The offshore team is not the problem. The gap is senior monitoring calibration.

The team works hard and can absorb a lot. RDI needs someone who can help them sharpen judgment without flattening their initiative. The Principal CRA should make the team more independent, not more dependent.

Current gap:

- Monitoring standards need to be made explicit and teachable.
- Judgment-heavy decisions need a senior escalation path.
- Real work needs to be reviewed against a common bar.
- The team needs examples of what good looks like, not just abstract SOPs.
- RDI needs a company-owned monitoring rubric that survives growth.
- The team needs help converting IVD-specific study risk into practical monitoring plans.
- Junior CRAs need a way to triage questions without flooding the senior reviewer with every issue as urgent.

What RDI can backstop:

- IVD context and RDI-specific operating model.
- Company priorities and escalation pathways.
- Sponsor expectations and internal systems.
- Final judgment on role fit and standard.

What RDI cannot backstop for long:

- Weak monitoring craft.
- Inability to teach.
- Insecurity around offshore talent.
- Cultural rigidity.
- Rigid checklist thinking that does not transfer across protocols.
- A senior person who needs authority because of title rather than usefulness.
- Someone who writes documents but does not change real work.
- Someone who cannot learn diagnostics and sample-focused studies quickly.
- Someone who cannot prioritize when many junior team members ask questions at once.

The failure mode is a senior CRA who keeps the hard work for themselves, complains that the offshore team "does not get it," produces SOP-like documents that do not improve actual monitoring files, or treats every unusual IVD question as a reason to escalate instead of reason from the study's objective, protocol, consent, sample requirements, and data requirements.

## Evaluation Inputs

Evaluate only based on job-relevant information from:

- Resume
- Application answers
- Written screening answers
- Interview transcript or notes
- Practical monitoring exercise
- Work sample or training sample, if provided
- References, if available

Do not infer or score based on protected characteristics, age, race, ethnicity, religion, disability, family status, marital status, gender, sexual orientation, national origin, appearance, accent, or other non-job-related personal information.

When evaluating cross-cultural fit, focus only on job-relevant behavior: coaching style, respect for offshore colleagues, ability to work across time zones, clarity of communication, handling of different communication norms, and willingness to transfer knowledge without ego.

When evaluating portability, do not use nationality or background as a proxy. Use evidence. The question is whether the candidate can adapt from one protocol or therapeutic pattern to fast-changing IVD studies, not where they are from.

## Decision Bands

### 85-100: Strong Advance

Candidate shows strong evidence of senior monitoring judgment, IVD portability, teaching ability, written standards-building, respectful cross-cultural coaching, and ability to triage many questions without becoming a bottleneck. Move to paid practical exercise or serious working session.

### 72-84: Advance / Pressure-Test

Candidate is plausible and worth testing. One or two areas need proof through work sample, live review, or targeted questions.

### 60-71: Hold / Only Advance if Pipeline Is Thin

Candidate has useful monitoring experience but lacks clear evidence of teaching, portability, IVD-specific thinking, or offshore/team-calibration fit.

### Below 60: Reject

Candidate is unlikely to raise the team's standard without creating more dependency, friction, or oversight burden.

A candidate can score well numerically and still be rejected for a hard gate issue.

# Hard Gates

## Must-Have Gates

- Deep clinical monitoring experience.
- Strong SDV/source review, protocol compliance, deviation, query, and site-management judgment.
- Can explain the why behind GCP/ICH and ALCOA+, not just cite rules.
- Can reason from the regulation, protocol, consent requirements, and data purpose rather than applying arbitrary judgment.
- Evidence of teaching, mentoring, onboarding, co-monitoring, oversight, or making other monitors better.
- Clear writing ability.
- Willing and able to hold 3-4 hours of daily overlap with UTC+8.
- Comfortable working with offshore colleagues as capable peers.
- Willing to keep a small live caseload.
- Able to learn new study types quickly rather than relying on years of repetition on one protocol.
- Able to triage many junior-team questions without either ignoring people or becoming the bottleneck.

## Strong Preference Gates

- Has built or improved monitoring standards, rubrics, playbooks, training materials, or review templates.
- Has reviewed other CRAs' monitoring work and given developmental feedback.
- Has worked with offshore, remote, global, or cross-cultural teams.
- Has IVD, diagnostics, lab, sample collection, clinical chemistry, hematology, immunoassay, molecular diagnostics, sample stability, device, or instrument-focused study experience.
- Has strong written examples that are plain, practical, and usable.
- Has experience developing monitoring plans from scratch or adapting monitoring focus to study-specific risk.

## Reject or Heavy Discount

Reject or heavily discount candidates who:

- Are only interested in being an individual contributor monitor.
- Want a people-management or Director-of-Monitoring role.
- Mainly want this job to stop traveling but do not show teaching or portability.
- Cannot show evidence of teaching other monitors.
- Write in generic SOP language that does not help real work.
- Are threatened by offshore talent or lower-cost team structures.
- Talk down to offshore colleagues, junior staff, coordinators, or site staff.
- Treat communication-style differences as competence problems.
- Need title authority to be respected.
- Are too removed from hands-on monitoring.
- Cannot hold UTC+8 overlap.
- Cannot communicate clearly in writing.
- Over-index on therapeutic area familiarity instead of monitoring judgment.
- Confuse workload management with craft development.
- Rely on checklist answers without explaining the underlying regulatory or study-specific reason.
- Say "throw out the sample" or "include it" on a consent issue without analyzing regulatory requirements, protocol, site corrective action, documentation, and defensibility.
- Treat sample stability studies as patient-safety monitoring exercises instead of sample/instrument/data-integrity exercises.

## 2. IVD Portability and Sample-Focused Thinking — 15 points

Evaluate whether the candidate can switch from traditional patient/visit-centered monitoring to IVD sample-focused monitoring.

Many traditional pharma CRAs are trained to monitor patient safety, drug accountability, AE/SAE reporting, and protocol visits. That experience is useful, but IVD studies often shift the center of gravity. In many RDI studies, the primary risk is not subject safety. It is whether the sample, storage conditions, transport, testing window, instrument, and resulting data are valid for the sponsor's intended analytical or clinical claim.

### Score 14-15

Candidate shows strong evidence of:

- Quickly understanding study-specific objectives.
- Building monitoring focus around sample validity, stability, storage, chain of custody, temperature logs, timing windows, instrument use, calibration/QC, test runs, data traceability, and protocol-defined collection/handling requirements.
- Distinguishing patient-safety risk from sample/data-integrity risk.
- Creating or adapting a monitoring plan from scratch.
- Learning new protocols quickly.
- Explaining IVD monitoring priorities without needing years of repetition on one protocol.

### Score 9-13

Candidate likely can adapt but needs pressure-testing with an IVD scenario.

### Score 4-8

Candidate has solid traditional CRA experience but limited evidence of sample-focused or instrument/data-focused thinking.

### Score 0-3

Candidate appears too rigid, patient-visit-centric, or protocol-repetition-dependent for RDI's study mix.

### Green Flags

- IVD, diagnostics, device, lab, central lab, or sample stability experience.
- Talks about sample collection, storage, shipping, temperature control, stability windows, chain of custody, accessioning, instrument logs, calibration/QC, test result traceability, specimen IDs, and protocol-defined handling.
- Can explain how monitoring priorities change by study objective.
- Has built or modified monitoring plans.

### Red Flags

- Answers every IVD question with generic GCP or patient-safety language.
- Cannot say what is critical to monitor in a sample stability study.
- Treats samples as ordinary source data rather than the core study object.
- Needs a sponsor-provided checklist to know what to monitor.
- Has spent years repeating one protocol and shows little evidence of adaptation.

## 6. Senior Escalation and Consent/GDP Judgment — 10 points

Evaluate whether the candidate can own the hardest monitoring questions without over-escalating, under-escalating, or making arbitrary calls.

The consent question is a key screen because it tests whether the candidate can reason from regulatory requirements and documentation principles instead of making a reflexive sample-in/sample-out decision.

### Score 9-10

Candidate shows strong evidence of:

- Handling significant protocol deviations.
- Informed consent or eligibility concerns.
- Persistent site noncompliance.
- Data integrity issues.
- Query disputes.
- Monitoring-plan ambiguity.
- Sponsor/site escalation.
- CAPA-related judgment.
- Knowing when to escalate, to whom, and with what recommendation.
- Defending a decision later using regulation, protocol, source, consent requirements, and documented rationale.

### Score 6-8

Candidate has escalation experience but may need pressure-testing on judgment and communication.

### Score 3-5

Candidate has handled routine issues but limited evidence of hard judgment.

### Score 0-2

Candidate is not credible as senior escalation point.

### Consent Question Calibration

Use this scenario:

> The CRA found a consent form signed by the subject but with the printed name missing. It is a very rare patient for this sample collection study. Do you include the subject? Why or why not? If QA challenged your decision later, how would you defend it?

Strong answer should include:

- Do not make an arbitrary "throw out the sample" decision.
- Do not make an arbitrary "include the subject because the patient is rare" decision.
- First determine the regulatory, protocol, IRB-approved consent form, and site SOP requirements.
- Recognize that a valid informed consent generally hinges on required signature/date elements and documented informed consent process, not necessarily the printed-name field alone unless the approved form/procedure makes it required.
- Identify the missing printed name as a documentation/GDP issue and a site quality signal.
- Check whether the subject's identity is otherwise clear from the signed consent packet, subject ID, date, source, enrollment log, and related documentation.
- Query/site-follow-up to correct the incomplete documentation if allowed by site SOP/IRB/process, with proper late-entry or correction practice.
- Assess whether the consent was actually obtained before any study procedure.
- Assess whether the issue affects subject identification, consent validity, sample traceability, or audit defensibility.
- Document the rationale and escalation path.
- If keeping the subject/sample, defend it by showing the required consent elements were present, the process occurred before collection, identity/traceability were clear, the missing printed name was corrected or documented as a GDP deficiency, and QA/sponsor were informed if required.
- If excluding the subject/sample, defend it only if the missing printed name creates an actual regulatory/protocol/IRB/SOP failure or unresolved identity/traceability problem.

Weak answer:

- "Throw out the sample."
- "Include it because the patient is rare."
- "It is fine because there is a signature."
- "Ask QA and do whatever they say."
- Does not distinguish consent validity from documentation completeness.
- Does not reference regulation, protocol, IRB-approved form, site SOP, timing, correction process, or defensibility.

### Green Flags

- Presents issue, risk, options, and recommendation.
- Escalates early but not theatrically.
- Documents well.
- Distinguishes critical, major, minor, and trend issues.
- Knows when to involve PM, sponsor, QA, PI, IRB, or site leadership.
- Can defend a nuanced decision later.

### Red Flags

- Escalates everything.
- Escalates too late.
- Gives problems without recommendations.
- Hides uncertainty.
- Makes unsupported calls.
- Treats escalation as blame.
- Makes sample inclusion/exclusion decisions from instinct rather than regulatory/protocol reasoning.

# High-Signal Screening Questions

Use these questions to collect high-signal evidence before a serious interview. Select 3-5 based on resume gaps.

## 1. Consent / GDP Judgment

Ask exactly:

> The CRA found a consent form signed by the subject but with the printed name missing. It is a very rare patient for this sample collection study. Do you include the subject? Why or why not? If QA challenged your decision later, how would you defend it?

What this tests:

- Whether they reason from regulations, protocol, IRB-approved form, site SOP, and documentation standards.
- Whether they distinguish consent validity from documentation completeness.
- Whether they protect sample value without being reckless.
- Whether they can defend a nuanced decision later.
- Whether they can see a GDP/site-quality signal without overreacting.

Strong answer:

- "I would not decide based only on rarity or only on the missing printed name."
- "I would verify required consent elements, timing, identity, traceability, and protocol/IRB/SOP requirements."
- "A missing printed name is a documentation issue and may be a GDP signal, but a signed consent may still be valid if required signature/date/process elements are present and identity is otherwise clear."
- "I would query the site and correct/document according to GDP and site process if allowed."
- "I would include only if I can defend consent validity, subject identity, sample traceability, timing, and documentation correction."
- "I would exclude or escalate if the missing field creates actual consent invalidity, identity ambiguity, unresolved traceability risk, or protocol/IRB/SOP noncompliance."

Weak answer:

- "Throw out the sample."
- "Include it because rare patients are hard to find."
- "It is fine because there is a signature."
- "I would ask QA."
- No regulatory or protocol reasoning.
- No distinction between illegal/invalid consent and incomplete documentation.

## 2. Sample Stability Monitoring Plan

Ask exactly:

> What is critical to monitor in a sample stability study?

What this tests:

- Whether they can create a monitoring plan from scratch for IVD.
- Whether they understand that the study center of gravity is sample validity, storage, timing, instrument, and data traceability.
- Whether they default to generic patient-safety monitoring.

Strong answer should mention:

- Study objective and endpoint: what stability claim is being supported.
- Sample type and collection requirements.
- Time zero definition.
- Collection time, processing time, storage start time, testing time, and allowed windows.
- Storage conditions: temperature range, room/fridge/freezer, freeze-thaw limits, light exposure, tube/container type, preservatives/additives.
- Temperature logs, excursions, alarm response, and excursion documentation.
- Chain of custody and sample traceability from subject to storage to test run.
- Labeling, accessioning, sample ID reconciliation, and aliquot tracking.
- Instrument identity, calibration, maintenance, QC, reagent lots, operator training, and run validity.
- Protocol deviations tied to timing, handling, storage, instrument, or data capture.
- Data integrity: raw result, calculated result, audit trail, transcription, and source-to-database consistency.
- Criteria for excluding or flagging samples.
- Monitoring frequency based on risk, not just routine visit schedule.

Weak answer:

- Focuses mostly on AEs, SAEs, concomitant meds, general eligibility, and visit windows.
- Says "follow the protocol" without naming what stability-specific risks to monitor.
- Does not mention sample storage, timing, temperature, chain of custody, instrument, QC, or traceability.
- Cannot articulate how the monitoring plan differs from a traditional drug study.

## 3. Junior CRA Triage

Ask exactly:

> How will you manage 8 junior CRAs all sending you urgent questions?

What this tests:

- Whether they can juggle and prioritize.
- Whether they can support an eager offshore team without becoming overwhelmed.
- Whether they can create a triage system instead of functioning as a help desk.
- Whether they understand that junior staff may label too many things urgent because they are trying hard and do not yet have calibration.

Strong answer should mention:

- Establishing escalation categories: patient/subject safety, consent, sample viability, data integrity, site blocking issue, sponsor-impacting issue, routine question.
- Requiring minimum context: study, site, protocol section, source evidence, proposed answer, deadline, risk if no answer.
- Triage by risk, time sensitivity, reversibility, sample loss, consent/data integrity, and whether a site is blocked.
- Quick-response lane for true urgent issues.
- Daily office hours or scheduled review block for non-urgent questions.
- Shared question log or decision library.
- Turning repeated questions into rubric updates and training examples.
- Coaching CRAs to bring recommendations, not just problems.
- Avoiding both extremes: ignoring questions or answering everything immediately.

Weak answer:

- "I would answer them in order."
- "I would tell them to only message me if urgent."
- "I would have them ask their manager."
- "I am good at multitasking."
- No triage criteria.
- No system for repeated questions.
- No teaching loop.

## 4. Monitoring Judgment

Tell us about a monitoring issue where the right answer was not obvious from the SOP. What was the issue, what facts did you need, what did you decide, and how did you document or escalate it?

Strong answers should include:

- Specific issue.
- Relevant facts.
- Candidate's actual judgment.
- Risk assessment.
- Escalation path.
- Documentation.
- Outcome.
- Why the decision mattered.

Weak answers:

- "I followed the SOP."
- No specific facts.
- No explanation of risk.
- Escalated without recommendation.
- Cannot explain outcome.

## 5. Teaching Through Real Work

Describe a time you made another CRA or clinical team member better at monitoring. What were they missing, how did you teach it, and how did you know they improved?

Strong answers should include:

- Specific skill gap.
- Real work reviewed.
- Feedback method.

# Practical Work Sample for Finalists

Use only for finalists or serious candidates.

## Scenario

Give the candidate a de-identified sample monitoring file or mock packet containing:

1. A monitoring report with vague follow-up language.
2. A query log with several weak or leading queries.
3. A signed consent form with missing printed name.
4. A sample stability log with timing windows and one temperature excursion.
5. A chain-of-custody record with one ambiguous handoff.
6. Instrument/QC documentation that may or may not support the relevant run.
7. A site follow-up email that is polite but does not clearly close the issue.
8. A Slack/Teams-style list of urgent questions from 8 junior CRAs.

## Candidate Task

Ask the candidate to provide:

1. A short review of what they would flag.
2. Which issues are critical, major, minor, or training-only.
3. Whether they would include, exclude, query, or escalate the consent/sample issue and why.
4. What is critical to monitor in the sample stability portion.
5. Feedback they would give to the CRA who prepared the file.
6. A draft rubric section or example that would prevent the issue next time.
7. How they would triage the 8 junior CRA questions.
8. What they would leave alone.

## Evaluation Criteria

Strong candidates will:

- Separate critical issues from minor issues.
- Explain why each issue matters.
- Avoid over-escalating.
- Avoid arbitrary sample exclusion.
- Reason from regulation, protocol, consent requirements, site SOP, sample validity, and data integrity.
- Understand sample stability as a sample/instrument/data problem, not mainly a patient-safety problem.
- Give feedback that improves the CRA, not just the file.
- Write practical, plain standards.
- Show respect for the person whose work they are reviewing.
- Produce something RDI could reuse.
- Triage junior questions by risk and urgency.

Weak candidates will:

- Nitpick everything equally.
- Miss the highest-risk issue.
- Throw out valuable samples without defensible rationale.
- Include questionable samples only because they are rare.
- Confuse documentation incompleteness with automatic invalidity.
- Rewrite the file but not teach the CRA.
- Use dense regulatory language.
- Escalate without recommendation.
- Sound condescending.
- Produce an SOP instead of a usable standard.
- Treat 8 junior questions as a multitasking problem rather than a system problem.

# Core Decision Rule

Advance candidates who can credibly say:

> I can raise the monitoring bar by reviewing real work, teaching the judgment behind the standard, and building tools the team uses without me.

Reject candidates who are only one of the following:

> I am a strong monitor, but I do not really teach.

or

> I like training, but I am not current enough in monitoring craft.

or

> I can manage a team, but I do not want to be the senior IC doing live review.

or

> I am comfortable with offshore teams only when they stay in a subordinate execution lane.

or

> I can follow a monitoring checklist, but I cannot build the monitoring logic from the protocol, sample requirements, instrument requirements, and study objective.

For RDI, the Principal CRA must be technically credible, plainspoken, teacher-minded, fast-learning, and secure enough to make a smart offshore team stronger.
