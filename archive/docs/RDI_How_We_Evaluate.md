# How We Evaluate People at RDI

*For Conall and Lara — and for an LLM asked to evaluate a candidate against our job description and role-specific rubric. Sections 1-5 are how we think. Section 6 is how we run hiring and who does what. Section 7 is what an LLM should produce.*

---

## How to use this document

Use this document to evaluate candidates against RDI's actual operating needs, not generic role expectations. A candidate is not being judged as an abstract person. They are being judged as a possible complement to Conall, Lara, and the current team.

The core question is:

> **What load does this person remove, what risk do they reduce, what gap do they hand back, and can RDI cover that gap?**

This file is the company-wide evaluation philosophy. Do not turn it into a role-by-role scorecard or a dumping ground for every current team weakness. Current team context belongs in the role-specific rubric. This document defines how RDI thinks. The role rubric defines what RDI needs removed for that seat.

Before evaluating a candidate, read both:

1. This master "How We Evaluate People at RDI" file.
2. The role-specific rubric.

The master file governs the method. The role rubric governs the trade: weights, gates, work sample, current-team gap, and output format.

---

## 1. The headline

Conall is RO-5. Lara is COO. They are the permanent judgment layer for now. No hire at RDI's current stage changes that. So we are not hiring replacements. We are hiring **complements**.

That means we evaluate the **gap, not the person**. A candidate's absolute quality matters less than the shape of what they add on top of us. The role is downstream of the person: we do not simply fill a fixed slot, we decide which burden to put down first and shape the seat around the person who can carry it.

We refuse only what we cannot backstop. Technical depth can sometimes be taught. Honesty, humility, and coachability cannot. Those are hard gates.

This is not a quirk of how Conall and Lara run things. It is the defining condition of RDI's stage. In the Churchill & Lewis growth model, RDI is a textbook **Success-Growth (Stage III-G)** company: growing fast, owner still doing top-stratum work, bringing on first dedicated professional layers while staying deeply involved. The owner is still synonymous with the business, and the first serious hires extend the owner's reach rather than replace the owner's judgment.

Evaluating for a complement is exactly what this stage requires.

---

## 2. The one question everything serves

> **How much of Conall and Lara does this person take off the plate, what do they hand back, and is what they hand back a gap we can cover?**

There are two axes hiding in that question, and they are not the same purchase:

- **Work off the desk** — operational relief. The technician who takes a class of work off Conall or Lara so they stop touching it.
- **Risk off the company** — founder-dependency removed. The owner who becomes the credible judgment layer for a function so Conall or Lara is not the only person who can hold it.

The second is worth more than it scores because it is the kind of key-person risk a buyer prices. The best hire reduces the load whose gap is cheapest for us to keep covering, on the steepest upward curve, at the lowest price for the risk they remove.

### Where current-team weaknesses belong

Current team weaknesses should be explicit, but not in this master document.

Put them in the role-specific rubric as the **Existing Team Context / Gap to Fill** section. That section should answer:

- What is currently sitting on Conall or Lara that should not be?
- What does the current team do well?
- What does the current team not yet hold independently?
- What can Conall and Lara backstop?
- What can they not afford to backstop?
- What failure mode would create more work instead of less?

The master file tells the evaluator how RDI thinks. The role file tells the evaluator what burden the candidate is being hired to remove.

## 3. The method: read actions, including the ones not taken

We do not evaluate claims. We evaluate **choices**, because choices are made at a level, with consequences, and they reveal work-level, judgment, motivation, and character at once.

What someone says they want is noise. What they did is signal.

Actions are everywhere in the file:

- What they chose to write in an application answer.
- Whether they gave the concept or hid behind a brand name.
- What they chose to claim on the resume.
- Whether the resume survives contact with their public professional profile.
- What school, major, first job, city, industry, or function they chose.
- How they used gaps.
- Why they left each role.
- Whether their choices show deliberate ownership or drift.

And the move most screeners miss: **an omission is an action**.

The MBA left off the application. The salary number withheld as "market." The achievement that should be on the resume and is not. The concept that should have been in the answer and was replaced by a tool name. A keyword match sees what is on the page; we see what someone chose to keep off it. That is the edge.

---

## 4. The reads

These are not four separate interviews. **Topgrading produces the action history; the other reads interpret that same history through different lenses.** Run in this order.

### 1. Topgrading — the life story is the data set

Walk the chronology and read every choice as a decision made at a level.

Did they choose deliberately and own the outcome, or did they drift and get acted upon? Did each move make sense against a coherent ambition, or is the career a set of explanations after the fact? Where did they stretch? Where did they retreat? Where did they take risk? Where did they choose comfort?

The story is where work-level actually shows because it is all real choices with real stakes.

### 2. RO / level — what level do they operate at?

Read time-span of discretion:

- Do they think in tasks?
- Do they think in projects?
- Do they think in functions?
- Do they think in company-level outcomes?

Read it off the register:

- Task-declaratives: "Prepared, reconciled, approved, coordinated, supported" with no outcome often point to Level II.
- Owned-function language: "Built, owned, led, turned around, created, ran the audit, owned the close, trained the team, carried the client" points to III+ if the evidence supports it.

This sizes them against Conall as RO-5. A three-stratum gap creates chronic compression: we would specify everything, they could not take intent, and we would supply the judgment anyway.

### 3. Motivation — inferred from actions, never from stated wants

Why this seat, read from what they did.

Someone who found the role and cold-reached us reads differently from someone drifting between fractional gigs. Someone who keeps choosing founder-led chaos reads differently from someone leaving structured corporate support for the first time. Someone who repeatedly chose craft reads differently from someone repeatedly chased title.

The action discloses the motive. The cover letter does not.

### 4. Integrity and humility — also from actions

These are the two we cannot backstop.

Not vibe. Choices.

- What they put in an answer.
- What they claimed versus what is true elsewhere.
- Whether they owned a weak spot or dressed it up.
- Whether they gave direct evidence or inflated language.
- Whether they respect people below them, beside them, and outside their status lane.

Integrity and humility are hard gates because more of our time can never fix them.

### 5. Trajectory x our multiplier

People are an investment, not a fixed quantity bought at a price.

The question is not only "what can they do today?" It is:

> What is the slope, and does working under an RO-5 CEO and a strong COO bend it up?

A coachable Level III with integrity can appreciate under us. A rigid one is flat or depreciates. Integrity, humility, and coachability are what make the multiplier positive. The deeper reason they are hard gates is that without them nothing compounds and we are just renting the starting capability.

### 6. Salary is a vector, not a ceiling

Price only means something against trajectory and load.

- **$250K and truly a judgment layer** is not automatically a budget rejection. It may be a fundraise decision if the person removes top-stratum risk.
- **$90K and great in two years** is a discount on future value if we can bend the curve.
- The metric is dollars per unit of load removed over the horizon we will hold them.

Cheap-to-cover gap plus steep positive trajectory beats a high headline number. Expensive but real risk-removal may still be worth it.

## 5. Founder-mode and sellability are the same discipline

We intend to run RDI in founder-mode and keep it sellable. That is not a contradiction. It is the objective.

What suppresses enterprise value is not the founder being deeply involved. It is the founder being the only place certain judgment lives. A buyer discounts **key-person risk**, not hands-on leadership.

So the goal is:

> **Founder-mode at the top of the stack, zero single points of failure underneath.**

We stay the ceiling on vision, taste, and standards on purpose. We engineer out any operation, number, workflow, or client dependency that dies when Conall or Lara leaves the room.

This is why an owner complement can be worth more at exit than a higher-scoring technician. Board-credible finance that does not require Conall to be the CFO removes exactly the kind of key-person risk that compresses a multiple. A clinical operations lead who trains the team to operate without Lara in every detail does the same. An EA who removes Conall's open loops does the same at the attention layer.

Churchill & Lewis names the trap precisely. The Stage III-G to **Take-Off (Stage IV)** transition is where the binding factor flips from "owner's ability to do" to **delegation, people, and systems**.

Owners stall in two ways:

- **Omniscience** — everything routes through them, so the bottleneck wears founder-mode's clothes.
- **Abdication** — they go hands-off with no controls and call it delegation.

Real founder-mode is the narrow path between them:

> **Delegate the doing, keep the standard, instrument the handoff.**

That discipline lets RDI be founder-led and still have a clean diligence file. It is why trajectory matters. The hire who fits III-G but cannot absorb real delegation is the one who breaks at Take-Off.

## 6. Who does what, and the people function we are building toward

The evaluation function is not one job. It is four layers of work at different levels. The leverage is putting each at the lowest level that can hold it, and keeping at the top only what genuinely cannot move down.

### Define the function — Conall + Lara, irreducible for now

Which burden we put down first, what we will backstop, how steep a curve we will bet on, the salary-as-vector calls, and the per-role weights. This is RO-5 work. It changes per candidate and the weights live in our heads. It does not delegate at our stage.

### Gates and final call — Conall + Lara, always for now

Integrity, ego, coachability, and the verdict. These stay with us because they are the irreversible decisions.

### Run the reads — the system does this now

Gather the actions and omissions. Diff resume against public profile. Draft the verifiable candidate profile. Surface the `[NEEDS YOU]` items. This is the delegable leverage, and today an LLM running this rubric does it so our judgment lands only on the gates and the call.

### Logistics — push down now, own by a hire later

Sourcing, pipeline, scheduling, candidate experience, ATS hygiene, offers, onboarding. This is the most delegable layer and the first thing a people hire eventually takes off us. Until then it goes to the offshore admin pillar and EA support, not to Conall or Lara.

## When we hire a People person

The trigger is not a headcount number. It is when recruiting becomes the bottleneck on RO-5 work.

Three signals, any two together:

- Reqs are concurrent and steady, not episodic.
- The logistics layer is eating Conall/Lara hours that should go to clients, board, BD, operations, or Diagnostica.
- RDI is crossing into Take-Off, where people and systems must be acquired ahead of growth, not after.

Rough band: a first dedicated People seat usually lands somewhere in the 60-100 employee range, but for RDI, volume may trip it before headcount because of the Philippines expansion and multiple pillars hiring at once.

### What kind of hire

Not a CHRO.

At this stage it is a **People Ops / Talent execution hire** at roughly Stratum II-III. They own sourcing, logistics, first-pass reads using this rubric, hiring compliance, and Kaptan/EOR coordination.

This hire is itself a complement, not a replacement. It takes the doing off our plate and hands back the weights and calls, which stay with Conall and Lara for a long while. A senior VP People who owns the weights is a Stage IV-to-V hire, not now.

### What has to be true first

- Steady req volume justifies a full-time seat.
- ATS discipline exists.
- The function is documented so the hire executes a known method instead of freelancing their own.

That last point is the reason this rubric exists in writing. It turns the eventual handoff into inheriting a documented function, not absorbing tribal knowledge.

---

## The middle — what we do until then

This is now.

- The LLM runs the reads against this rubric as the stand-in for the People hire's first pass.
- Conall and Lara keep function-definition, gates, and calls. Nothing else.
- Delegable logistics go offshore now: scheduling, sourcing ops, candidate comms, ATS hygiene, and document collection.
- Format: use live working sessions and paid trial days when possible. They are higher-signal than generic interviews or broad async take-homes.
- Build junior internally when the role allows it, instead of chasing senior external hires that cost more screening bandwidth than we have.

This is the same discipline from Section 5 turned on hiring itself. Recruiting is founder-dependency too. We engineer it down now with the system and offshore admin, and structurally later with a People hire. Even then, we keep the weights at the top and delegate the doing.

The day we cannot do that, we have recreated the single point of failure we are trying to remove.

## 7. Operating instructions for an LLM evaluating a candidate

Given the job description, this master rubric, the role-specific rubric, the candidate's resume, application answers, public professional profile, and any screen or work sample, produce a candidate profile built for Conall to **verify**, not redo.

### Required method

- Reason from Sections 1-6.
- Frame the whole evaluation around the question in Section 2.
- State which burden the person buys down.
- State whether they are a technician complement, owner complement, operator complement, teacher complement, logistics complement, or unclear.
- Read actions and omissions.
- For every inference, cite the specific action or non-action it rests on.
- Run the reads: topgrading, RO/level, motivation, integrity/humility, trajectory x multiplier, and salary-as-vector.
- Write every inference as a claim with evidence and a confirm/reject for Conall.
- Pull anything unverifiable to the top as `[NEEDS YOU]`.
- Keep dimensions separate. Do not blend fit, evidence quality, integrity, level, trajectory, and compensation into one generic vibe.
- Treat integrity and ego as gates, not scores.
- Test portability. Do not reward coincidence.
- The verdict is last and explicitly Conall's call.

### Required output framing

Every candidate profile should start by naming the trade:

- **Burden removed:** What work, judgment, or risk this candidate plausibly takes off Conall, Lara, or the current team.
- **Gap handed back:** What still needs to be covered if we hire them.
- **Backstoppable or not:** Whether that gap is acceptable at RDI's current stage.
- **Complement type:** Technician complement, owner complement, operator complement, teacher complement, or logistics complement.
- **Gate issues:** Any integrity, ego, coachability, compensation, schedule, or logistics issue that could override the rest.

Do not begin with generic strengths. Begin with the RDI trade.

### Compliance

Use job-relevant evidence only. Never extract, infer, or flag protected or non-job attributes including age, race, national origin, religion, gender, sexual orientation, disability, health, family status, photos, appearance, or accent.

When discussing team context, keep it job-relevant. It is acceptable to evaluate whether a candidate can work across time zones, coach offshore teammates, respect high-performing lower-cost colleagues, handle direct feedback, and teach without ego. Do not evaluate protected traits or use nationality, ethnicity, age, gender, religion, family status, health, disability, appearance, or accent as proxies for fit.

Public and async text is self-reported and may be AI-written. Treat polish as weak evidence. Push the load onto live, unscripted demonstration and practical work sample where possible.

The output is not meant to decide for Conall. It is meant to hand Conall three or four real decisions and the evidence to make them in minutes.
