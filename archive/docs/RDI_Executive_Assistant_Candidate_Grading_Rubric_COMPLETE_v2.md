# RDI Executive Assistant Candidate Grading Rubric

## Purpose

Use this rubric to evaluate candidates for the Executive Assistant to the CEO role at RDI based on their resume, application answers, written responses, interview notes, and practical writing sample.

This role is not a general administrative assistant role, office manager role, recruiter role, Chief of Staff role, or strategy role. It is a high-trust, high-ownership Executive Assistant role supporting one demanding CEO directly.

The core question is:

> **Will this person reduce the CEO's cognitive load within 30 days, or will they become another person the CEO has to manage?**

A good EA does not merely complete assigned tasks. A good EA reduces reminders, decisions, rework, missed follow-ups, context switching, and preventable interruptions. The output is not a clean task list. The output is a CEO with fewer open loops.

---

## Role Context

RDI is a small, fast-moving IVD-focused CRO. The CEO's time is the company's most constrained resource. This role exists to protect that time.

The EA will own:

- CEO calendar management and gatekeeping
- CEO inbox triage and selected responses
- Meeting prep and background briefings
- Follow-up tracking and chasing commitments to closure
- Drafting emails and short communications in the CEO's voice
- Logistics for sponsor meetings, client visits, travel, and leadership meetings
- Coordination across leadership on shared timelines
- Occasional personal logistics, expenses, errands, and practical support

This role requires someone who wants to be excellent at executive support. It is not a stepping stone into strategy, Chief of Staff, HR, recruiting, or general operations.

---

## Existing Team Context / Gap to Fill

RDI does not need a generic admin. RDI needs someone who gives Conall time and attention back immediately.

Conall's bottleneck is not only calendar volume. It is the number of open loops, half-routed conversations, follow-ups, reminders, introductions, interview logistics, client scheduling issues, investor/admin tasks, and practical frictions that require his attention because no one else is fully holding them.

The EA is being hired to reduce cognitive load, not create a new management lane.

The candidate must be able to:

- Protect Conall's calendar without needing every prioritization rule explained.
- Triage communication and surface what actually matters.
- Chase follow-ups without making Conall remember to chase the chaser.
- Draft in a direct, plainspoken voice.
- Notice loose ends and close them.
- Handle mundane work without status friction.
- Work with Lara, Marielle, Nabbe, Kaptan/offshore admin, and other internal operators without turning coordination into drama.
- Become more useful every week by learning Conall's patterns.

The failure mode is an EA who is pleasant, organized, and hardworking but still needs Conall to manage the work. That person adds coordination load instead of removing it.

The strongest candidate is a logistics-and-judgment complement. They do not need to be strategic in the abstract. They need to reliably make the CEO less burdened.

## Evaluation Inputs

Evaluate only based on job-relevant information from:

1. Resume
2. Application answers
3. Written screening answers
4. Video transcript or interview notes, if provided
5. Writing sample, if provided
6. References, if available

Do not infer or score based on protected characteristics, age, race, ethnicity, religion, disability, family status, marital status, gender, sexual orientation, national origin, health, appearance, or any non-job-related personal information.

Do not penalize candidates for career gaps unless the candidate's explanation directly creates a job-related concern, such as inability to commit to an in-office full-time role.

---

## Overall Recommendation Categories

Assign one recommendation.

### Strong Advance

Candidate should move to serious interview, reference check, or practical trial if the screen is already complete.

Use this only when the candidate shows strong evidence of direct principal support, calendar/inbox/follow-through ownership, clear writing, and low-ego execution fit.

### Advance

Candidate is plausible and worth testing further through tailored questions or a practical writing prompt.

Use this when the candidate has relevant experience but one or two areas need clarification.

### Hold / Maybe

Candidate has some useful signal but is missing important evidence or has unresolved concerns.

Use this when the pipeline is thin or the candidate may be useful as a backup.

### Reject

Candidate does not show enough evidence for this specific role or appears misaligned with the role's actual demands.

Reject if the candidate is mainly an office manager, general admin, HR/recruiting coordinator, team assistant, remote-only EA, aspiring Chief of Staff, or status-oriented candidate without strong execution evidence.

|---|---:|

| Score | Meaning |
|---:|---|
| 85-100 | Strong Advance |

A candidate can score well numerically and still be rejected if there is a hard-gate issue.

## Hard Gates

These should usually be required before advancing.

### Required or near-required

- Has meaningful EA or senior executive support experience
- Has supported one CEO, founder, managing partner, owner, or similarly demanding principal
- Has direct calendar ownership experience
- Has evidence of inbox, drafting, follow-up, logistics, or meeting prep ownership
- Can work full-time in person in Van Nuys / Los Angeles area if required
- Is legally authorized to work in the United States, if this is a stated role requirement
- Compensation expectations are not obviously incompatible with the role
- Can reduce founder cognitive load, not merely complete assigned admin tasks

### Founder-Load Gate

Usually reject or heavily discount if the candidate cannot show evidence of reducing a principal's cognitive load. Calendar management alone is not enough. Look for ownership of prioritization, follow-up, drafting, routing, preparation, and closing loops.

### Usually reject if

- Mostly office manager experience without true principal support
- Mostly admin coordinator experience without senior executive ownership
- Mostly HR/recruiting/coordinator background
- Mostly team assistant supporting many executives shallowly
- Wants a Chief of Staff, strategy, operations, or leadership-track role more than an EA role
- Remote-only pattern or clear reluctance toward in-office work
- Cannot show examples of calendar ownership, inbox management, follow-up tracking, or executive writing
- Application answers are vague, generic, or overly polished without evidence
- Writing is poor, inflated, evasive, or not usable
- Candidate appears status-conscious, luxury/celebrity-adjacent, or star-struck without low-ego execution evidence
- Needs constant clarification on routine executive-support decisions
- Treats every ambiguity as something to escalate
- Sends polished updates but does not actually close the loop
- Requires Conall to remember what they are supposed to remember
- Wants the role because of proximity to the CEO more than because they want to own executive support

# Category Scoring Details

For each category, assign a 1-5 subscore, then convert to the weighted score.

Weighted score formula:

> category weighted score = (subscore / 5) x category weight

## 1. Single-Principal Executive Support — 20 points

This is the most important resume filter.

The role requires deep support for one demanding principal, not shallow support across a large executive team.

### 5 - Excellent

- Primary EA to CEO, founder, managing partner, owner, or equivalent
- Supported one principal deeply and daily
- Clearly owned the principal's workflow, schedule, communications, and follow-up
- Has worked in a demanding, high-trust executive relationship
- Experience appears directly transferable to supporting Conall

### 4 - Strong

- Has meaningful senior executive support experience
- May have supported more than one executive but shows deep ownership of one principal
- Some evidence of close daily access and judgment

### 3 - Mixed

- Supported executives but unclear depth of ownership
- May have been part of an executive admin team
- May have supported several executives but not deeply owned one principal

### 2 - Weak

- General admin, coordinator, office manager, HR, or operations support with limited principal ownership
- Executive support exists but appears shallow or occasional

### 1 - Poor

- No meaningful executive support experience
- No evidence of supporting a single demanding principal

### Evidence to look for

Strong phrases:

- Primary EA to CEO
- Executive Assistant to Founder
- Supported Managing Partner
- Right hand to CEO
- Managed CEO calendar and inbox
- Prepared principal for meetings
- Drafted correspondence on behalf of CEO
- Tracked executive follow-ups

Weak phrases:

- Supported executive team
- Provided administrative support
- Coordinated calendars
- Assisted department heads
- Office operations
- Helped with special projects

---

## 2. Calendar, Inbox, and Follow-Through Ownership — 20 points

This role exists to protect the CEO's time and prevent commitments from being dropped.

### 5 - Excellent

Candidate has owned most or all of the following end-to-end:

- Complex calendar management
- Calendar gatekeeping and prioritization
- Inbox triage
- Drafting or sending emails on behalf of principal
- Meeting prep
- Follow-up tracking
- Chasing internal and external commitments
- Travel and logistics
- Action item management

### 4 - Strong

- Strong calendar and logistics ownership
- Some inbox, communication, or follow-up ownership
- Likely capable with some ramp

### 3 - Mixed

- Good scheduling and logistics experience
- Limited evidence of inbox, drafting, or chasing commitments
- May need meaningful direction

### 2 - Weak

- Mostly scheduling, booking, expenses, or admin tasks
- Little evidence of judgment or ownership

### 1 - Poor

- No clear evidence of calendar, inbox, or follow-through ownership

### RDI-specific interpretation

For RDI, follow-through is more important than scheduling polish. A candidate who can organize a complex calendar but cannot chase commitments to closure will fail. The best signal is repeated evidence that a principal stopped worrying about a category of work because the candidate owned it completely.

Look for examples where the candidate:

- Caught a missed commitment before the executive did.
- Prepared the executive before a meeting without being asked.
- Reprioritized a calendar based on business context.
- Routed a request to the right person and confirmed completion.
- Followed up across internal and external stakeholders until done.
- Created a simple system that made reminders unnecessary.

## 3. Writing, Communication, and Judgment — 15 points

This EA must be able to write in the CEO's voice: short, direct, practical, and clear.

### 5 - Excellent

- Resume and answers are crisp, specific, and low-fluff
- Candidate has drafted executive communications, client emails, board materials, memos, or sensitive messages
- Written answers show judgment about what to include and omit
- Writing is usable with little editing
- Tone is direct and professional, not corporate sludge

### 4 - Strong

- Clear writing with some evidence of executive drafting
- Mostly concise and specific
- Some editing may be needed but foundation is good

### 3 - Mixed

- Writing is adequate but generic
- Some fluff or over-explanation
- Limited proof of writing on behalf of a principal

### 2 - Weak

- Vague, wordy, corporate, inflated, or evasive writing
- Repeats buzzwords without evidence
- Writing sample would need major rewriting

### 1 - Poor

- Poor writing mechanics, unclear thinking, or unusable tone

### Strong writing style for this role

- Short sentences
- Clear ask
- Direct recommendation
- Practical context
- No performative warmth
- No long apology spirals
- No vague business language

### Weak writing style

- "I thrive in fast-paced environments" without example
- "I am passionate about supporting leaders" without evidence
- Long paragraphs that hide the point
- Too much emotional language
- Too polished but empty

## 4. Speed, Ambiguity, and Practical Execution — 15 points

RDI is small, fast, and not process-heavy. The EA must operate without perfect instructions.

### 5 - Excellent

- Has worked in founder-led, startup, private office, professional services, healthcare, biotech, clinical, legal, investment, real estate, or high-pressure client service environments
- Shows ability to make practical decisions quickly
- Can sequence priorities under pressure
- Has examples of fixing problems before the principal sees them
- Does not need spoon-feeding

### 4 - Strong

- Some evidence of high-paced or ambiguous environments
- Has handled competing priorities and last-minute changes well

### 3 - Mixed

- Can probably execute but mostly from structured environments
- May need adjustment to RDI's pace and ambiguity

### 2 - Weak

- Background is heavily bureaucratic or process-defined
- Answers suggest they wait for instructions or approval

### 1 - Poor

- No evidence of working in ambiguity or under pressure
- Appears likely to freeze, escalate too much, or need constant direction

### Strong evidence

- Last-minute travel/logistics fixes
- Managed rapid changes
- Supported CEO during fundraising, growth, crisis, launch, event, or transition
- Ran meeting logistics end-to-end
- Prioritized executive schedule under pressure
- Solved operational friction independently

## 5. Humility / Low-Ego Service Orientation — 15 points

This is a hard cultural filter.

The right candidate is not star-struck, status-conscious, delicate, or seeking glamour. They value usefulness over visibility.

### 5 - Excellent

- Comfortable doing unglamorous work well
- Shows pride in service, precision, and reliability
- Has handled both high-trust work and mundane tasks without resentment
- Does not need ceremony, status, or constant praise
- Takes direct feedback cleanly
- Wants to make the principal and company more effective, not become the center of attention

### 4 - Strong

- Generally low-ego and practical
- Some evidence of doing whatever was needed
- No obvious status-seeking concerns

### 3 - Mixed

- Says the right things but examples are thin
- May be fine but needs pressure-testing
- Could be more polished than gritty

### 2 - Weak

- Seems attracted to proximity, prestige, luxury, brand names, celebrities, or executive access
- Emphasizes being "in the room" more than making things run
- May view mundane work as beneath them

### 1 - Poor

- Status-oriented, high-maintenance, or obviously misaligned with a spartan execution culture
- Wants visibility, strategy, glamour, or lifestyle more than support execution

### RDI-specific interpretation

RDI is not a prestige EA role. There is no ceremony here. The candidate needs to be comfortable supporting a high-intensity CEO while also doing ordinary practical work well. Low ego does not mean passive. It means useful, direct, unoffended, and focused on outcomes.

Strong candidates do not need glamour. They take pride in making the system work.

### Green flags

- "No task too small" backed by real examples
- Has handled errands, logistics, room setup, scheduling messes, travel fixes, and follow-ups without drama
- Describes the work in practical terms
- Shows respect for building staff, vendors, internal teams, and junior people
- Has supported demanding people without turning it into personal drama

### Red flags

- Over-indexing on luxury, celebrity, entertainment, VIP, red carpet, fashion, or high-status environments
- Resume reads like a lifestyle brand
- Mentions impressive names but little ownership
- Wants to be a strategic partner without showing EA fundamentals
- Seems offended by direct feedback or practical tasks
- Application answers are polished but empty

Important nuance: entertainment, luxury, private-client, or high-profile experience is not automatically bad. Some candidates from those environments are excellent. The key distinction is whether they are a high-discretion, low-ego operator or a status-adjacent personality hire.

- Shows ownership and resilience
- Has dealt with difficult executives, urgent timelines, or operational chaos and remained useful
- Has examples of improving after feedback

### 4 - Strong

- Some evidence of resilience and persistence

### 3 - Mixed

- May have worked hard, but examples are generic
- Some short tenure or ambiguity requiring clarification

### 2 - Weak

- Repeated short stints without good explanation
- Frames every difficulty as someone else's fault
- Little evidence of staying through hard periods

### 1 - Poor

- Appears brittle, blame-oriented, or likely to quit when the role becomes unglamorous or difficult

### Strong evidence

- Multi-year tenure in demanding roles
- Stayed through transition, acquisition, growth, crisis, fundraising, leadership change, or high-volume period
- Learned from mistakes
- Can describe direct feedback and behavioral change
- Does not overpersonalize hard environments

## Candidate Pattern Recognition

### Best-fit profile

The strongest candidate likely has:

- 5+ years as EA to one CEO, founder, managing partner, principal, or owner
- Calendar and inbox ownership
- Executive writing/drafting experience
- Follow-up tracking and chasing experience
- Fast-paced or high-pressure environment experience
- Low ego and comfort with unglamorous work
- Stable career pattern or credible reasons for transitions
- Strong in-person logistics fit
- Direct, clear written communication

### Dangerous false-positive profile

Be careful with candidates who:

- Look impressive on paper because of high-status companies or people
- Interview well and are polished
- Talk about strategy, access, executives, events, or lifestyle
- Have weak evidence of inbox/calendar/follow-through ownership
- Have not supported one principal deeply
- Want the EA role as a stepping stone
- Need a lot of validation or do not handle direct feedback well

### Underrated strong profile

A candidate may be strong even without flashy brands if they:

- Have supported one demanding owner/founder/principal for years
- Handled everything from inbox to logistics to personal errands
- Write clearly
- Show calm, practical judgment
- Have strong references from the principal
- Have low-ego service orientation

Application answers should be judged for evidence, not polish.

### Strong answers

- Specific examples
- Clear ownership
- Practical sequence of action
- Shows judgment and prioritization

# Key Screening Questions to Generate for Candidates

After evaluating the resume and application answers, generate 2-4 tailored questions that clarify the main uncertainty about the candidate.

Do not ask all candidates the same questions. Use the resume to identify the concern.

## If candidate supported multiple executives

Ask:

> Your background shows support across multiple executives. This role is deep support for one principal. Can you walk me through the closest experience you have had owning one executive's calendar, inbox, follow-up, and meeting prep end-to-end?

## If candidate appears office-manager heavy

Ask:

> Your background appears to blend office management and executive support. This role is primarily direct support to the CEO: calendar, inbox, writing, meeting prep, and follow-through. What percentage of your prior work was direct principal support, and what exactly did you own?

## If candidate appears Chief of Staff / strategy-oriented

Ask:

> This role is not a Chief of Staff or strategy role. It is a high-trust executive support role focused on calendar, inbox, writing, logistics, and follow-through. Why is this specific EA role the right fit for what you want next?

## If candidate has high-profile / luxury / entertainment background

Ask:

> Your background includes high-touch or high-profile environments. RDI is more spartan: fast pace, direct feedback, practical execution, and no ceremony. What makes you confident this environment is a fit, and what parts of that shift would be hardest?

## If candidate has short tenures

Ask:

> Can you briefly walk me through why you joined and left your last three roles? I am mainly trying to understand the pattern in your career moves.

## If candidate has unclear writing experience

Ask:

> Have you drafted emails or communications directly on behalf of a principal? What kinds of messages were you trusted to draft without approval, and what still required approval?

## If candidate has strong resume but unclear humility / grit

Ask:

> This role includes high-trust executive support, but also plenty of unglamorous work: chasing follow-ups, fixing logistics, rescheduling messy calendars, handling errands, and making sure small things do not become CEO problems. Tell me about a role where you had to do important but unglamorous work consistently. What did that look like?

## If candidate may be sensitive to direct feedback

Ask:

> Tell me about a time you received direct feedback from a demanding executive or manager. What was the feedback, how did you respond, and what changed afterward?

## If candidate has big-company background

Ask:

> Your background includes larger or more structured organizations. RDI is smaller, faster, and less process-heavy. What makes you confident you would be effective in that environment?

## If candidate is earlier-career

Ask:

> Where would you still need training or ramp time in this role, and where do you believe you could be immediately useful?

## If candidate has strong EA experience but unclear cognitive-load reduction

Ask:

> Give a specific example of a category of work your principal stopped thinking about because you took it over. What was happening before, what did you change, and how did you keep it from coming back onto their plate?

## If candidate has calendar experience but unclear prioritization judgment

Ask:

> Tell me about a time you had to decide what should get on a principal's calendar and what should not. What information did you use, and how did you handle pushback?

## If candidate has follow-up experience but unclear ownership

Ask:

> Describe the system you used to track follow-ups for a busy executive. How did you make sure commitments actually closed instead of just being logged?

## If candidate appears polished but may not be practical

Ask:

> This role includes drafting, calendar triage, meeting prep, errands, rescheduling, chasing people, and fixing small messes before they become CEO problems. Which parts of that work have you personally owned, and which parts would be new?

## Practical Writing Prompt

Use this with candidates who pass resume review.

> Please draft a short email from Conall to a client who asked for a meeting next week, but his calendar is overloaded. The tone should be direct, professional, and concise.

Evaluate the response for:

- Brevity
- Clarity
- Directness
- Practical alternative offered
- No over-apologizing
- No corporate fluff
- Sounds like a busy CEO, not HR or legal

Strong example:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I would like to connect, but next week is tight. Could we look at the week after, or if this is time-sensitive, send me the main question and I will respond directly or route it to the right person internally.
>
> Best,
> Conall

Weak example:

> Dear [Name],
>
> Thank you so much for reaching out. Unfortunately, due to a very busy calendar and competing priorities, Conall is unavailable next week. We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience this may cause and would be happy to coordinate an alternative time that works for everyone.
>
> Warm regards,
> [Candidate]

The weak example is too corporate, too apologetic, and too long.

## Async / Screen Recommendation Logic

After scoring, decide the next step.

### Advance if

- Score is 72+
- No hard gate failure
- Candidate has enough direct EA/principal support evidence
- The main concerns can be clarified through 2-4 questions or a writing prompt

### Hold if

- Score is 60-71
- Resume has some relevant signal but important gaps
- Pipeline is thin
- Candidate may be useful as backup

### Reject if

- Score is below 60
- No single-principal support evidence
- No calendar/inbox/follow-through ownership
- Strong strategy/Chief of Staff mismatch
- Clear logistics mismatch
- Strong status-oriented or low-humility concern
- Writing is poor or vague

## Example Calibration

### Example: Generic but competent application answer

Candidate answer:

> I would understand the need, compare options, check reviews and pricing, present choices, and handle ordering and setup.

Interpretation:

- This is competent but low-signal.
- It shows basic professionalism but not exceptional ownership.
- Do not advance based on this alone.
- Advance only if the resume shows strong direct EA experience.

Stronger version would include:

- Number of chairs
- Budget
- Ergonomics
- Timeline
- Approval threshold
- Delivery and assembly
- Return policy
- Vendor comparison
- Who needs to be consulted
- How to avoid wasting the CEO's time

### Example: Generic building maintenance answer

Candidate answer:

> I would thank them, review the situation, communicate to the team, put a simple process in place, and maintain the relationship.

Interpretation:

- Acceptable but generic.
- Stronger answer would inspect the issue, identify the source, fix the behavior, communicate a clear rule, and close the loop with building staff.

## Important Judging Principles

1. Evidence beats adjectives.
2. Specific examples beat polished claims.
3. Single-principal ownership beats broad administrative exposure.
4. Low-ego execution beats status proximity.
5. Direct writing beats corporate polish.
6. Career pattern matters more than one impressive job.
7. Do not confuse event planning or office management with executive support.
8. Do not confuse Chief of Staff ambition with EA excellence.
9. Do not advance candidates who will require heavy management to become useful.
10. The best candidate is probably not the most impressive candidate. It is the candidate who has already done this job for a demanding principal and still wants to do it.
11. Do not advance a candidate because they are polished. Advance them because they have already made a demanding principal's life operationally lighter and can prove it with specific examples.
