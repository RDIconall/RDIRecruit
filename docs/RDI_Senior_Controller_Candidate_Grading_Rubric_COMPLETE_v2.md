# RDI Senior Controller Candidate Grading Rubric

## Purpose

Use this rubric to evaluate candidates for RDI's Senior Controller role based on their resume, application answers, written responses, interview notes, and practical work sample.

This rubric is designed for use by an LLM or human reviewer. The goal is not to identify the most credentialed accountant. The goal is to identify the candidate most likely to own RDI's finance function end to end in a fast-moving, PE-backed, project-based clinical research business.

The strongest candidate can do two things at the same time:

1. Work directly with non-finance operators, executives, project teams, lab staff, and clients to extract the messy operational facts needed for accurate accounting.
2. Produce GAAP financials, revenue recognition, lender reporting, forecasts, and board materials that will survive auditor review and private equity board scrutiny.

The controller must be a bridge between operations and audit-ready financials. That bridge is the role.

---

## Role Context

RDI is an IVD-focused contract research organization. It runs diagnostic studies for sponsors and operates in a small, fast-moving, high-ownership environment.

The Senior Controller will own:

- Monthly close
- GAAP financial statements
- ASC 606 revenue recognition
- Project-level EAC / ETC tracking
- Billing tracker ownership
- Borrowing-base reporting
- Covenant compliance
- Lender reporting
- Forward forecast model
- Board financial reporting
- Oversight of outsourced accounting partner
- AP / AR / bookkeeping quality through the outsourced team
- Operational finance partnership with the CEO, COO, lab, project teams, and client-facing teams

Revenue is recognized study by study using a cost-based input method:

> Cumulative Actuals / EAC x Total Contract Value

Getting project cost, EAC, TCV, billing, and change order data right matters to the board, the lender, auditors, and the operating team.

The role is onsite in Van Nuys, five days per week.

---

## Existing Team Context / Gap to Fill

RDI does not need a bookkeeper. RDI needs someone who reduces founder dependency in finance.

Today, Conall can backstop board narrative, lender context, investor expectations, and the business judgment behind the numbers. That is not the same as owning the finance function. He should not be touching close mechanics, revenue recognition math, billing tracker maintenance, EAC discipline, audit schedules, or routine financial reporting cadence.

Lara can provide operational truth from projects, lab work, staffing, vendors, and client delivery. She should not have to translate every operational fact into accounting treatment. She also should not have finance slow down operations because the controller needs clean inputs handed over perfectly.

The controller is being hired to bridge that gap.

The candidate must be able to:

- Pull messy operational facts out of project teams, lab, BD, and leadership.
- Decide what facts matter for GAAP, ASC 606, EAC, billing, accruals, lender reporting, and board reporting.
- Hold the outsourced accounting partner accountable without becoming dependent on them.
- Build a finance rhythm that reduces CEO involvement over time.
- Tell Conall what changed, why it matters, and what decision is needed.

The failure mode is a controller who is technically competent but still requires Conall to be the real CFO. That person may reduce bookkeeping work, but they do not remove risk from the company.

The strongest candidate is an owner complement, not just a technician. A technician can run close tasks. An owner can make the finance function more trustworthy without Conall personally translating every operational fact into financial consequence.

---

## Core Hiring Thesis

The right candidate is not merely a technically competent accountant. The right candidate is an operational controller who can sit between Lara's real-world operating facts and Conall's board/lender/investor obligations, then produce financials that survive scrutiny without dragging the company into bureaucracy.

The hire works only if Conall gets out of the finance weeds. If Conall still has to explain every revenue recognition implication, chase every billing tracker issue, interpret every project overrun, or personally police the outsourced accounting partner, the hire has not solved the problem.

The candidate must be able to hear operational facts like:

- We bought laptops for new coordinators.
- Lara needs a fume hood for the lab.
- A client verbally approved additional work, but the change order is not signed.
- A project is over budget but not complete.
- Work has been performed but has not yet been invoiced.
- Lab supplies were purchased, but it is unclear whether they are study-specific or general overhead.
- A client has delayed approval, but RDI has already incurred costs.

And translate them into finance questions:

- Is this capex or expense?
- Is it project-specific or overhead?
- Is it billable or non-billable?
- Does it affect EAC?
- Does it affect ASC 606 revenue recognition?
- Does it change total contract value?
- Does it require a signed change order?
- Does it require an accrual?
- Is there adequate audit support?
- Should it appear in lender reporting or board reporting?
- Does the forecast need to change?

The best candidate will not wait for clean inputs. They will build a practical process to get the right inputs without slowing down the business.

## Overall Scoring

Score the candidate out of 100 points.

| Category | Weight |
|---|---:|
| GAAP / Close / Audit-Ready Financials | 20 |
| ASC 606 / Project Revenue Recognition / EAC Discipline | 20 |
| Operational Finance Translation | 20 |
| Board / Lender / PE-Quality Reporting | 15 |
| Ownership of Outsourced Accounting / Team Direction | 10 |
| Forecasting / Billing Tracker / Cash Visibility | 10 |
| RDI Fit: Grit, Humility, Onsite, Low-Ego Execution | 5 |
| **Total** | **100** |

### RDI-Specific Weighting Note

Do not let a candidate score well by being only technically strong. For RDI, operational finance translation is not a soft skill. It is core technical capability. A controller who cannot get accurate facts from non-finance people cannot produce accurate financials here.

When in doubt, overweight evidence that the candidate has personally turned messy operational inputs into audit-ready financial reporting.

---

## Decision Bands

### 85-100: Strong Advance

The candidate shows strong evidence of both technical controller capability and operational finance partnership. Move to serious interview or practical work sample depending on process stage.

### 70-84: Advance / Pressure-Test

The candidate is plausible and may be strong, but one or more areas need follow-up. Use tailored questions or a work sample focused on the exact gaps.

### 55-69: Hold / Only Advance if Pipeline Is Thin

The candidate has some relevant experience but lacks clear evidence for key parts of the role. Do not spend CEO time unless there is a specific reason.

### Below 55: Reject

The candidate is unlikely to own the role without significant oversight or ramp.

A candidate can score well numerically and still be rejected if there is a hard gate issue.

# Scoring Categories

## 1. GAAP / Close / Audit-Ready Financials — 20 points

Evaluate whether the candidate can produce financials that stand up to auditor review, lender reporting, and a private equity board.

This is not a bookkeeping role. The candidate must demonstrate technical precision, close discipline, balance sheet ownership, revenue recognition support, documentation quality, and the ability to defend the numbers.

### Score 18-20

Candidate shows strong evidence of:

- Owning or heavily leading monthly close under GAAP
- Preparing or reviewing GAAP financial statements
- Balance sheet reconciliations and flux analysis
- Audit schedules and audit support
- Technical accounting judgment
- Documentation that supports accounting conclusions
- Controls mindset without becoming slow or bureaucratic
- Experience in audited, lender-reporting, PE-backed, or board-accountable environments

### Score 12-17

Candidate shows solid accounting experience, but may not have fully owned the controller package. They may have handled important pieces of close, reporting, audit support, or reconciliations, but ownership depth is not fully clear.

### Score 6-11

Candidate appears more operational-accounting or bookkeeping-focused. Limited evidence of GAAP financial statement ownership, audit support, technical memos, balance sheet ownership, or external reporting.

### Score 0-5

Candidate cannot credibly produce audit-ready financials. They would require too much oversight from the CEO, auditors, external consultants, or outsourced accounting partner.

### Green Flags

- Controller, Assistant Controller, Accounting Manager, Senior Manager, or audit-to-industry progression
- CPA or strong public accounting foundation
- Audit support or audit readiness ownership
- Month-end close ownership
- GAAP financial statements
- Balance sheet reconciliation ownership
- PE-backed, lender-reporting, or board-reporting environment
- Demonstrated ability to explain accounting judgments

### Red Flags

- Mostly bookkeeping, AP, AR, payroll, or transactional accounting
- No clear close ownership
- No audit exposure
- Cannot explain judgment-heavy accounting issues
- Uses accounting jargon without showing ownership of support
- Appears dependent on external accountants for all technical conclusions

### Score 6-11

### Green Flags

- ASC 606
- EAC / ETC
- Percentage of completion

- Contract assets / contract liabilities
- Unbilled revenue / deferred revenue

## 3. Operational Finance Translation — 20 points

Evaluate whether the candidate can work directly with non-finance operators, executives, lab staff, project managers, and client-facing teams to gather the information needed to produce accurate financials.

This role requires someone who can translate messy operational facts into correct accounting treatment. The candidate must be able to ask practical questions, educate non-finance colleagues without condescension, and determine whether items affect expense classification, capitalization, project cost, billing, revenue recognition, accruals, lender reporting, or audit support.

This category is critical. A technically strong accountant who needs clean inputs handed to them will fail. A people-friendly operator who cannot produce audit-ready financials will also fail.

### Score 18-20

Candidate shows strong evidence of:

- Working directly with operations, project teams, lab teams, client-facing teams, or executives
- Gathering accounting inputs from non-finance people
- Translating accounting concepts into practical business language
- Handling ambiguous spend, project costs, capex vs opex, accruals, billable vs non-billable costs, or revenue recognition inputs
- Chasing documentation and clarifying facts without becoming bureaucratic
- Partnering with strong operators without slowing them down or talking down to them
- Understanding that accurate financials depend on understanding the business, not just reviewing the GL

### Score 12-17

Candidate has some cross-functional finance experience but mostly receives semi-clean inputs. They seem capable of working with operations, but examples are limited.

### Score 6-11

Candidate is mostly accounting-department focused. They have limited evidence of working directly with non-finance teams. They may be technically competent but reactive.

### Score 0-5

Candidate appears back-office only. They cannot show examples of gathering operational facts and would likely blame operations for bad inputs rather than building a process to get the right information.

### Green Flags

- Worked closely with operations, project management, lab, service delivery, client teams, procurement, or department leads
- Built processes to collect financial inputs from non-finance stakeholders
- Trained or coached non-finance teams on expense coding, purchase approvals, billing, project costs, or documentation
- Examples involving capex vs opex, accruals, project cost allocation, billable costs, or revenue inputs
- Calm, practical communication style
- Evidence of getting facts without creating unnecessary process drag

### Red Flags

- Talks only in accounting jargon
- Appears irritated by non-finance people not knowing accounting rules
- Needs clean inputs handed to them
- Reactive rather than investigative
- Would slow Lara or operations down
- Would produce financials disconnected from operational reality
- Cannot explain how they would turn messy operational information into accounting support

# Hard Gates

A candidate should normally not advance unless they pass these gates.

## Must-Have Gates

- Relevant progressive accounting experience
- Credible close / GAAP / audit-ready financials experience
- Some meaningful revenue recognition, project accounting, or services accounting exposure
- Willing and able to work onsite in Van Nuys five days per week
- Can communicate clearly in writing
- Does not appear purely transactional or bookkeeping-only
- Can reduce founder dependency in finance, not merely perform accounting tasks

## Strong Preference Gates

- CPA or strong public accounting equivalent
- Project-based revenue recognition experience
- EAC / ETC / percentage-of-completion or cost-based revenue exposure
- PE-backed, lender-reporting, audited, or board-facing experience
- Experience directing outsourced accounting or a small team
- Comfort working with non-finance operators

## Reject or Heavy Discount

Reject or heavily discount candidates who:

- Cannot credibly own monthly close
- Lack GAAP financial statement experience
- Lack meaningful revenue recognition or project accounting exposure
- Need clean inputs handed to them
- Cannot work with non-finance stakeholders
- Are purely back-office accountants
- Are too senior and hands-off
- Are too junior and need too much training
- Are remote-first or unwilling to work onsite
- Cannot produce clear written explanations
- Treat revenue recognition as simply matching invoices
- Have no evidence of audit support or external scrutiny
- Require the CEO to remain the real judgment layer behind close, revenue recognition, lender reporting, or board finance
- Treat operations as a source of bad inputs rather than the reality finance must understand
- Cannot explain how they would create a recurring operating cadence with Lara, PMs, lab, BD, and the outsourced accounting partner

# Application / Screening Questions

Use these questions to collect high-signal information before a serious interview. Do not ask all questions of every candidate if it creates too much friction. Select the most important 3-5 based on resume gaps.

## 1. Operational Finance Translation

Lara tells you the lab needs to buy a fume hood. She does not know whether it should be expensed, capitalized, allocated to a study, or treated as general lab overhead. What questions would you ask before deciding how to account for it?

Strong answers should mention some of:

- Cost
- Useful life
- Capitalization threshold
- Whether it is new equipment, a repair, replacement, or improvement
- Whether it creates future economic benefit
- Whether it is tied to a specific client or study
- Whether it increases capacity or simply maintains operations
- Installation, freight, setup, or related costs
- Required documentation and approval
- Depreciation treatment
- Forecast / cash impact
- Whether any portion is billable or reimbursable

Weak answers:

- "I would check the policy."
- "I would ask the accountant."
- "I would capitalize it because it is equipment."
- "I would expense it because it is for the lab."

## 2. Revenue Recognition / Change Order Judgment

A project manager says a client verbally approved additional study work, the team has started the work, but the signed change order is not back yet. What would you want to know before recognizing revenue, accruing cost, or including it in the billing tracker?

Strong answers should mention some of:

- Contract enforceability
- Whether written approval is required
- Scope and pricing
- Whether collectability is probable
- Whether work is distinct or part of an existing performance obligation
- Impact on TCV
- Impact on EAC
- Billing rights
- Documentation support
- Whether work should be accrued even if revenue is not recognized
- Materiality
- Escalation to CEO / board if significant

Weak answers:

- "I would wait for the signed change order."
- "I would recognize it because the client verbally approved it."
- "I would ask the project manager what to do."

## 3. Audit-Ready Judgment

Give an example of a judgment-heavy accounting issue you handled that needed to survive audit, lender review, board review, or external scrutiny. What was the issue, what support did you prepare, and what was the outcome?

Strong answers should include:

- Specific issue
- Candidate's actual role
- Technical reasoning
- Documentation prepared
- Review process
- Outcome
- What they learned or improved

Weak answers:

- Generic audit participation
- No specific judgment
- "The auditors accepted it" without explaining why
- Cannot describe support prepared

## 4. Working with Non-Finance Teams

Tell us about a time operations, project teams, sales, or client-facing teams were not giving finance the information needed for accurate reporting. What did you do to fix the flow of information?

Strong answers should include:

- Specific information gap
- Why it mattered
- How the candidate gathered facts
- Process or template created
- How they educated or aligned non-finance teams
- How they avoided becoming a bottleneck
- Resulting improvement

Weak answers:

- Blames operations
- Says they sent reminders but did not change the process
- No measurable or concrete improvement

## 5. Controller Ownership

This role owns the full finance function while directing an outsourced accounting partner. What work would you personally inspect every month, and what would you be comfortable delegating?

Strong answers should distinguish:

Personally inspect:

- Revenue recognition
- EAC / TCV / billing tracker
- Balance sheet reconciliations
- Cash and AR
- Close package
- Material journal entries
- Accruals
- Lender / covenant reporting
- Board materials

Delegate with review:

- AP processing
- Routine bookkeeping
- AR follow-up
- Initial reconciliations
- Expense coding
- Payroll coordination, depending on structure

Weak answers:

- Too hands-off
- Too detail-trapped on low-value tasks
- Cannot articulate review controls

# Tailored Question Generator

After reviewing the resume and application answers, generate 2-4 candidate-specific questions. The questions should resolve the biggest uncertainties before a serious interview.

## If candidate is audit / public-accounting heavy

Ask:

> Your background shows strong technical accounting or audit exposure. This role also requires working directly with operations to extract messy facts and turn them into accounting inputs. Can you give an example where you had to work with non-finance operators to get the information needed for accurate financials?

## If candidate is bookkeeping / transactional-accounting heavy

Ask:

> Your background shows day-to-day accounting ownership. This role also requires GAAP financials that can stand up to auditors, lenders, and a private equity board. What financial statement, close, audit, or technical accounting work have you personally owned?

## If candidate has project accounting but unclear ASC 606 depth

Ask:

> Your resume mentions project accounting. Please walk through how you have handled EAC, ETC, contract value, work performed, billing, and revenue recognition on a project-by-project basis.

## If candidate has PE-backed or board exposure but unclear operational finance

Ask:

> Your background includes investor, executive, or board reporting. How close were you to the operational inputs behind the numbers, such as project costs, billing timing, capex decisions, and department-level spend?

## If candidate is senior and may be too review-only

Ask:

> This role requires owning the full finance function, including detailed review of billing trackers, reconciliations, outsourced accounting work, and operational follow-up. What work would you expect to personally do versus delegate?

## If candidate lacks obvious outsourced partner management

Ask:

> Have you directed an outsourced accounting partner, offshore team, or small internal accounting team? How did you set expectations, review their work, and hold them accountable?

## If candidate has healthcare / clinical / lab exposure but unclear accounting depth

Ask:

> Your background appears relevant to healthcare or clinical operations. Please describe the most technical accounting work you personally owned, especially around close, revenue recognition, audit support, or financial statements.

## If candidate has strong FP&A but unclear controller depth

Ask:

> Your background shows forecasting or financial planning experience. This role is controller-first: close, GAAP financials, revenue recognition, billing tracker, reconciliations, and audit support. Which of those areas have you personally owned?

## If candidate has controller experience but unclear project-based revenue

Ask:

> Please describe the most complex revenue recognition environment you have worked in. How did revenue relate to contract value, work performed, costs incurred, billing milestones, or project completion?

## If candidate has large-company background

Ask:

> RDI is a smaller, faster company where the controller will have to get into the details and work directly with operators. What makes you confident you would thrive without the support structure of a larger finance organization?

## If candidate looks technically strong but founder-dependency risk remains

Ask:

> This role is meant to reduce CEO dependency in finance, not just execute accounting tasks. Please describe a finance function, close process, reporting package, or operating cadence you owned in a way that made the CEO or CFO less involved over time. What did you take off their plate, what still escalated to them, and how did that change over time?

## If candidate has outsourced accounting experience but unclear ownership

Ask:

> RDI has an outsourced accounting partner, but the controller owns the standard. Walk through how you would structure the monthly cadence with an outsourced accounting team: what they prepare, what you inspect, what you reject, what you escalate, and what you report to the CEO.

## If candidate has project accounting exposure but unclear operational translation

Ask:

> Give a concrete example where project or operations information changed the accounting result. What did the non-finance team tell you, what did you ask next, and how did it affect revenue, margin, billing, accruals, or forecast?

# Serious Interview Questions

Use these after the candidate passes the initial screen. The live interview should focus on judgment, ownership, and fit, not a generic resume walk-through.

## Technical / Close / Audit

1. Walk me through the monthly close process you have owned. What were the key deadlines, reconciliations, reviews, and outputs?
2. Which balance sheet accounts do you personally review every month, and what are you looking for?
3. Tell me about the hardest audit issue you have owned. What support did you prepare?
4. What makes a close package board-ready instead of merely internally acceptable?
5. How do you know when a number is technically correct but operationally misleading?

## Revenue Recognition / Project Accounting

1. Walk me through your experience with ASC 606.
2. Have you worked with EAC, ETC, cost-to-cost, percentage-of-completion, or similar project revenue models?
3. How would you validate project EACs if project managers are optimistic or inconsistent?
4. What is the difference between work performed, revenue recognized, and invoice issued?
5. How would you handle a project that is over budget but not yet complete?

## Operational Finance Translation

1. How do you get accurate financial inputs from people who do not speak finance?
2. Tell me about a time operations created an accounting problem. What did you do?
3. How would you work with Lara if she needs quick practical guidance but does not want finance to slow down operations?
4. What accounting concepts do non-finance people most often misunderstand?
5. How do you educate operators without sounding bureaucratic or condescending?

## Outsourced Accounting / Team Direction

1. How have you managed outsourced accounting partners or small teams?
2. What do you inspect personally every month?
3. What would you delegate to the outsourced partner?
4. How do you handle repeated errors from an outsourced accounting team?
5. When would you bring work in house versus keeping it outsourced?

## Board / Lender / Forecast

1. What financial information does a PE board need that an internal operator may not need?
2. Have you prepared lender reporting, borrowing base reporting, or covenant compliance?
3. How do you explain forecast changes to a CEO or board?
4. What are the early warning signs that a project-based services business is drifting financially?
5. How would you connect billing tracker, revenue recognition, collections, and forecast into one operating view?

## Fit / Ownership

1. Why do you want to own a finance function rather than support one?
2. What kind of CEO brings out your best work?
3. What kind of environment frustrates you?
4. Tell me about a time you had to fix a messy process without waiting for permission.
5. What work in this role would you not want to do personally?

# Practical Work Sample for Finalists

Use only for finalists, not broad top-of-funnel candidates.

## Scenario

RDI is closing the month. You receive the following facts:

1. A lab fume hood was purchased for $18,000, plus $2,000 installation.
2. Lara says it will be used for multiple studies over the next several years.
3. A project manager says a client verbally approved $45,000 of additional work, but the change order is not signed.
4. The team has already incurred $12,000 of labor and supply cost related to that additional work.
5. One study has cumulative actual costs of $220,000, current EAC of $400,000, and TCV of $600,000.
6. The PM now believes EAC should increase to $475,000 because recruitment is slower than expected.
7. The outsourced accounting partner booked several supply purchases to general lab expense, but some may be study-specific.

## Candidate Task

Ask the candidate to provide a short memo covering:

- What questions they would ask
- What they would review before close
- Potential accounting treatment for the fume hood
- How they would think about the unsigned change order
- How the EAC change affects revenue recognition
- What they would ask the outsourced accounting partner to clean up
- What they would flag to the CEO before issuing financials

## Evaluation Criteria

Strong candidates will:

- Avoid jumping to unsupported conclusions
- Ask practical operational questions
- Understand capex vs opex
- Understand EAC impact on revenue
- Distinguish revenue recognition from billing
- Think about documentation and audit support
- Think about lender/board implications
- Communicate clearly without overcomplicating

Weak candidates will:

- Make unsupported accounting conclusions
- Ignore documentation
- Confuse billing with revenue
- Ignore EAC change impact
- Ignore project-specific cost classification
- Produce generic textbook answers

# Core Decision Rule

Advance candidates who can credibly say:

> I can get the messy truth out of operations and turn it into financials that auditors, lenders, and a PE board will trust.

Reject candidates who are only one of the following:

> I am good with people but light technically.

or

> I am technically strong but need clean accounting inputs handed to me.

or

> I can close the books, but the CEO still needs to be the real finance judgment layer.

For RDI, the controller must be both operationally useful and technically defensible. If they cannot do both, they are not the right fit.
